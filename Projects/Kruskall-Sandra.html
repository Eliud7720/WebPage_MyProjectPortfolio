Comparative analysis of the oral microbiota in patients with different degrees of autism and healthy controls

About this project

Autism is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition that primarily affects how individuals perceive the world, process information, communicate, and interact with others. According to the DSM-5, autism is classified into three levels based on the amount of support a person requires. Numerous studies have highlighted significant differences in the gut microbiota between individuals with autism and neurotypical controls. However, the oral microbiota has been far less explored in this context. To address this gap, a study was conducted analyzing the oral microbiota of 60 participants, including patients across different levels of autism and neurotypical individuals. The dataset consisted of the bacterial species counts for each participant, which now require statistical analysis to derive meaningful conclusions.

Objective

The objective of this analysis is to evaluate the provided dataset, which contains bacterial species counts for each participant, in order to identify species that are distributed differently across autism levels 1, 2, and 3, as well as neurotypical controls. This study focuses exclusively on the species-level breakdown, without presenting aggregated or higher-level analyses, and no additional analyses will be included.

Development

Data collection

The dataset used for this statistical and data analysis was provided by the requester in the format shown in the following figure. In this structure, each column represents a sample, while each cell corresponds to the count of a bacterial species for a given patient.

Exploratory Data Analysis

The data analysis and statistical procedures were performed using R. First, the total number of unique species in the dataset was counted, resulting in 1,859 species. Next, species that were absent across all patients were filtered out. This was done by summing across rows to determine the total abundance of each species in all samples. If the total sum for a species was zero, it would be discarded; however, in this case, every species had at least one occurrence. This step is necessary because, given how the dataset was generated and summarized, it may include species from external sources not actually present in the study samples.

Although these data now allow us to compare group medians, this alone is not sufficient to determine whether there are statistically significant differences between groups. To assess this, a statistical test such as ANOVA is required. However, before applying ANOVA, it is necessary to determine the distribution type of each variable. To this end, the Shapiro-Wilk test was performed in R to assess whether the data for each species follow a normal distribution. The Shapiro-Wilk test uses the null hypothesis that the data come from a normal distribution. If the resulting p-value is less than or equal to 0.05, there is a low probability that the data follow a normal distribution, and the null hypothesis is rejected, indicating a non-parametric distribution. Conversely, if the p-value is greater than 0.05, there is a reasonable probability that the data follow a normal distribution.

Since all species followed a non-normal distribution and there are four independent groups, the most appropriate test to determine differences between groups in this case is the Kruskal-Wallis test.

Kruskall Wallis test

The Kruskal-Wallis test was conducted to determine whether at least one of the groups exhibited a different distribution for each bacterial species. This analysis was performed using R, and the results were saved as a CSV file.

A total of 125 species were identified with a p-value less than 0.05, indicating a high probability that at least one of the groups exhibits a unequal distribution for each species. Additionally, a metric called difference was calculated for each species, measuring the disparity between the group with the highest mean abundance and the group with the lowest mean abundance to assess the practical significance of these differences. This step was necessary because, among the 125 species, many showed statistically significant but minor differences (e.g., median differences of 5 or 10), which, although statistically significant, are not practically relevant in this context.

Post-hoc analysis

Only species with practically relevant differences were retained. Subsequently, a Dunn post-hoc test was conducted to assess all pairwise group comparisons and determine if there were significant differences between groups. This analysis was performed using R, and the results were saved as a CSV file. The data were then analyzed, and only those cases where at least one group was truly different from the others were retained for further consideration.

At the conclusion of the post-hoc analysis, only 8 species were identified as having considerable significant differences in at least one group. These species are: Abiotrophia defectiva, Corynebacterium matruchotii, Selenomonas infelix, Streptococcus sanguinis, Actinomyces odontolyticus, Haemophilus sputorum, Neisseria polysaccharea, and Streptococcus lactarius. The following boxplots display the distribution of these species individually for each group (1, 2, 3, and controls).

From the previous plots, the most notable distributions were observed for Selenomonas infelix, showing a clear unequal distribution between neurotypical controls (C) and the patient groups (1, 2, and 3), and for Actinomyces odontolyticus, where the patients (1, 2, and 3) exhibited a considerably lower abundance compared to the neurotypical group. With this information, along with additional analyses performed at the genus, phylum, and group levels, these species can be further studied to explore their potential relationship with autism.

Conclusions

  • The oral microbiota of 60 participants, including patients with different levels of autism and neurotypical controls, was analyzed at the species level.
  • A total of 1,859 unique bacterial species were identified, all of which were present in at least one participant.
  • The Shapiro-Wilk test indicated that all species followed a non-normal distribution, justifying the use of non-parametric tests.
  • The Kruskal-Wallis test identified 125 species with significant differences (p < 0.05) between at least one group.
  • A difference metric was calculated to assess the practical significance of variations between groups, filtering out minor differences that were statistically significant but not meaningful.
  • A Dunn post-hoc test was applied to all pairwise group comparisons, retaining only species with at least one group showing a truly significant difference.
  • At the conclusion of the analysis, 8 species showed considerable significant differences: Abiotrophia defectiva, Corynebacterium matruchotii, Selenomonas infelix, Streptococcus sanguinis, Actinomyces odontolyticus, Haemophilus sputorum, Neisseria polysaccharea, and Streptococcus lactarius.
  • The most notable distributions were observed for Selenomonas infelix (unequal distribution between neurotypical controls and patient groups) and Actinomyces odontolyticus (patients had considerably lower abundance than neurotypicals).
  • These results, together with additional analyses at the genus, phylum, and group levels, provide a foundation to further explore the relationship between specific oral bacteria and autism.
